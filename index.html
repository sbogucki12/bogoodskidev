Most organizations don't have an AI problem. They have an enablement problem.

Every leadership team knows AI matters. The pressure is real. But knowing AI matters and actually operationalizing it inside a complex organization are two very different things.

Here's what usually happens instead:

  • ● Pilots that show promise but never scale past the demo.
  • ● Security and data concerns that stall every conversation.
  • ● Governance reviews that become bottlenecks instead of enablers.
  • ● No clear ownership for AI services, tooling, or cost.
  • ● Tool sprawl: teams buying or building independently with no coordination.
  • ● Employees experimenting faster than policy can keep up.
  • ● Leaders who can see the potential but can't find the path from interest to production.

These aren't technology failures. They're enablement failures. The model is rarely the hard part. The hard part is everything around it: access, data, cost, policy, security, training, ownership, and sustainment.

01

Start with the operating model

Before picking tools, figure out how AI services will actually be owned, funded, governed, and sustained in your organization. Strategy without an operating model is a PDF that nobody follows.

02

Make governance usable

Good governance should make safe progress easier, not impossible. I help design review and approval processes that protect the organization without freezing every initiative in place.

03

Build access patterns people can actually use

If getting access to an LLM takes six months and three committees, people will find workarounds. I help create access patterns that are secure, governed, and fast enough to be useful.

Demos are easy. Production is where enablement gets real. I focus on the full lifecycle: deployment, monitoring, cost tracking, performance management, and the organizational ownership that keeps things running.
