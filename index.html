Most organizations don't have an AI problem. They have an everything-around-AI problem.

Everyone knows AI matters. The pressure is real, the slides are flashy, the budgets are getting approved. But actually operationalizing this stuff inside a big, complicated organization is a totally different sport.

Here's what tends to happen instead:

  • ● Pilots that crush the demo and then quietly never scale.
  • ● Security and data questions that nobody can fully answer, so nothing moves.
  • ● Governance reviews that turn into bottlenecks instead of guardrails.
  • ● Nobody quite owns AI services, tooling, or the bill.
  • ● Tool sprawl, because every team quietly bought or built their own thing.
  • ● Employees are experimenting way faster than policy can keep up with.
  • ● Leaders can see the potential, they just can't find the path from "interested" to "in production."

None of these are technology failures. They're enablement failures. The model is rarely the hard part. The hard part is everything around it: access, data, cost, policy, security, training, ownership, and the unglamorous job of keeping the thing alive once it's live.

RAG Chatbot Program Design

Practical, technical, and built for actual institutional reality.

AI enablement isn't picking a model and calling it a day. It's process, architecture, security, data readiness, adoption, cost awareness, leadership trust, feedback loops, and somebody owning the thing in production. I work across all of it.

01

Start with the operating model

Before anyone picks tools, figure out how AI services are going to be owned, funded, governed, and kept alive in your organization. Strategy without an operating model is just a nicely formatted PDF nobody follows.

02

Make governance usable

Good governance should make safe progress easier, not turn every project into a hostage negotiation. I help design review and approval flows that protect the org without freezing every initiative on the runway.

03

Build access patterns people can actually use

If getting access to an LLM takes six months and three committees, your people will find a workaround. (They already have.) I help create access patterns that are secure, governed, and fast enough to not get bypassed.

04

Move from demos to actually-in-production

Demos are easy. Production is where enablement gets real. I care about the boring stuff: deployment, monitoring, cost tracking, performance, and the very specific human who gets paged when something breaks.
